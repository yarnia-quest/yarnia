# Founder Hackathon - Judging & Scoring Guide
**AI BEAVERS x Mollie founder hackathon Hamburg, House of AI — Saturday, June 6, 2026**

> Build something that looks like the beginning of a real startup, not only a polished hackathon project.

## What judges are looking for

1. A real problem with a specific customer
2. Evidence that the team understands the market, buyer, or user
3. A working product or credible prototype built during the hackathon
4. A clear pitch that shows why this team could keep going after June 6

A beautiful demo with no buyer logic will score lower. A rough but specific product with strong customer understanding can still do very well.

## Scoring rubric

| Criterion | Weight | What strong looks like |
|---|---|---|
| Problem and customer clarity | 20% | Names a specific customer or user, explains the pain clearly, avoids vague "everyone" markets |
| Market and business potential | 20% | Opportunity is plausible, buyer/distribution path makes sense, team can explain why this could become a real company |
| Product execution and demo | 20% | Built something concrete during the hackathon. Works enough to demonstrate the core idea |
| AI-native leverage and technical approach | 15% | AI used as meaningful capability, not decoration. Team can explain what was built |
| Evidence, insight, and founder edge | 15% | Real insight: customer knowledge, domain expertise, early validation, relevant data, or strong reason they are the right people |
| Pitch clarity | 10% | 3-minute pitch is clear, direct, judge-friendly. Covers problem, product, evidence, next step without buzzwords |

## Scoring scale

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 90–100 | Exceptional: clear startup potential, strong founder signal, credible product, sharp pitch |
| 70–89 | Strong: above average, real promise, some gaps but clearly worth attention |
| 50–69 | Solid: understandable and relevant, but missing sharpness, evidence, or execution depth |
| 30–49 | Early or unclear: interesting pieces, but major gaps in problem, product, market, or pitch |
| 1–29 | Not competitive: unclear, mostly generic, not sufficiently built, or hard to evaluate fairly |

## What counts as evidence

**Useful:**
- A specific customer or user segment, not a broad category
- A conversation, quote, pain point, or example from the target market
- Domain knowledge from the team's own work, study, network, or lived experience
- A small market scan: competitors, alternatives, current workflows, or why existing tools fail
- A working demo of the core workflow
- A GitHub repo with commit history from the day
- A clear next step: who you'd test with next, what you'd charge, what you'd build next

**Weak:**
- "The market is huge" with no customer detail
- "Everyone needs this"
- Generic AI trend slides
- A demo that looks good but doesn't prove the core use case
- Old work presented as if it was built at the hackathon

## How judging works on the day

- **Submission deadline: 19:00**
  - Required: public GitHub repo with README and commit history from the day
  - Required: pitch deck, max 7 slides
  - Optional: hosted preview URL

- **Preliminary round:** every team pitches in an assigned room — 3 minutes, no Q&A
- **Finalists:** ~6 teams
- **Final round:** 3 minutes pitch + 2 minutes Q&A per finalist
- Winners announced after the final round

## What does NOT matter as much

Don't over-optimize for:
- The prettiest slide deck
- A giant TAM slide with no buyer logic
- The most complicated architecture
- A perfect production-grade product
- A long feature list
- A generic AI wrapper with startup vocabulary attached

## Make your pitch judge-friendly

**Strong 3-minute structure:**
1. The customer and problem
2. What you built
3. Why now / why this matters
4. Evidence or founder edge
5. What you would do next

**Good first sentence shape:**
> "We help [specific customer] do [specific painful job] because today they are stuck with [bad current workaround]."

## Fairness and integrity

**Allowed:**
- Ideas, research, notes, sketches, and customer understanding prepared before June 6
- Generic templates, libraries, APIs, no-code tools, and open-source packages
- Existing domain knowledge and existing relationships

**Not allowed:**
- Presenting a mostly finished product as hackathon work
- Importing a mostly finished private project-specific codebase
- Faking repo history, demo functionality, users, revenue, or customer evidence
