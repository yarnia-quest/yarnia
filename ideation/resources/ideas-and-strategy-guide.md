# AI BEAVERS Founder Hackathon - Ideas & Strategy Guide

> Your job on June 6: name a painful buyer problem, build a narrow product that works, and show one piece of evidence that someone wants it.

## The hard talk

Most applications were **weak, not bad**. Generic ideas like "AI-powered job application automation" or "voice tutor for language learning" already have dozens of well-funded competitors.

> Almost every weak idea has a strong version hiding inside it. You usually don't need a new idea. You need a sharper buyer, a sharper wedge, a sharper "why now," and one piece of real evidence.

## How to find the right idea

**Start with yourself:**
- What did you study? What field do you work in? What industry do you know from the inside?
- What tools or workflows frustrate you or your colleagues every week?
- What problems have you watched real people struggle with — not read about, but seen up close?
- What changed recently that makes this possible or urgent now?
- Who can you text today to check whether this pain is real?

**Quick filter:** If your idea is a social media app, an AI therapist, or a generic dating app, you're competing with thousands of identical pitches. If the answer is "everyone could use this," narrow it until you can name the first 10 likely users.

## The 7 weak-idea patterns

1. **"ChatGPT for [industry]"** — thin wrapper, no proprietary data/workflow/distribution. Reframe: own a specific workflow inside a specific buyer's day.
2. **"AI [job title]"** — "AI lawyer," "AI accountant" with no named human whose job changes. Reframe: name the task, the tool it replaces, and the hour of the week.
3. **Feature, not company** — one clever workflow that Notion/Linear ships in 6 months. Reframe: show the wedge that grows into a system of record.
4. **Big number, no plan** — "$50B market" with no customer detail. Reframe: describe your first 10 customers by name, city, and pain.
5. **Solution looking for a problem** — "cool agent framework, looking for a vertical." Reframe: start from pain you've personally watched someone curse at this month.
6. **Generic horizontal "for everyone"** — "AI assistant for SMBs." Reframe: pick one beachhead narrow enough to list every prospect on one screen.
7. **Second-mover with no edge** — "like Harvey, but cheaper." Cheaper is not a strategy. Reframe: find what the category leader structurally cannot do.

## Three before/after reframings

### 1. SaaS challenger — kill a legacy product
**Weak:** "An AI-powered project management tool."

**Strong:** "Construction companies in Germany pay €800/seat/month for Procore (designed in 2002). We rebuilt the core workflow — daily logs, RFIs, punch lists — from scratch with AI. Our system auto-generates the daily log from site photos and voice notes. No data entry. €80/seat. Why now: AI collapsed the cost of building software by 10–100x."

### 2. Software for agents — not humans
**Weak:** "An AI agent platform for businesses."

**Strong:** "Every CRM, ERP, and HRIS was built for humans clicking buttons. AI agents manage pipelines and update records through brittle browser automation. We rebuild the CRM as an agent-native API layer: MCP endpoints, structured documentation, programmatic access agents can discover without a human in the loop. Why now: the next trillion users on the internet are not people — they are agents."

### 3. AI-native service — replace the service, not the tool
**Weak:** "An AI copilot that automates accounting tasks for SMBs."

**Strong:** "We are the bookkeeper. A D2C founder doing €1.2M on Shopify pays €400/month and still spends 4 hours fighting Lexoffice before every filing deadline. We connect to Shopify, the bank feed, and DATEV, auto-classify every transaction into SKR03, and export a clean file the Steuerberater signs off on. €99/month. Why now: Germany's e-invoicing mandate (ZUGFeRD/XRechnung) forced B2B transactions into structured formats from January 2025."

**The pattern:** name a specific person in a specific moment of pain, start with the narrowest wedge someone would pay for this week, and be honest about the risk that could kill the idea.

## Reality check — 7 questions before June 6

Answer each in under 30 seconds. Honestly.

1. **Wedge** — Can I describe what we do in one sentence without "platform," "ecosystem," or "AI-powered"?
2. **Buyer** — Can I name the exact human who signs the invoice — their role, company size, and country?
3. **Demand** — Would someone be genuinely upset if this disappeared tomorrow? Not "interested" — upset. Money is demand. Panic when it breaks is demand.
4. **Status quo** — What are people doing right now to solve this, even badly? If "nothing," the problem probably isn't painful enough.
5. **Why now** — Is there something impossible or illegal in 2023 that makes this work in 2026?
6. **Founder-market fit** — Have I personally lived this pain, or do I have an unfair relationship with people who have?
7. **Evidence** — Has a stranger said "take my money" — or at least "when can I try it" — in writing, this month?

## Stress-test with AI before June 6

Paste your idea into Claude, then ask it to walk through these one at a time (tell it to push back, not be nice):

- "What is the strongest evidence that someone actually wants this — not 'is interested,' but would be genuinely upset if it disappeared?"
- "What are people doing right now to solve this problem, even badly? What does that workaround cost in hours or money?"
- "Name the actual human who needs this most. What is their title? What gets them promoted? What gets them fired?"
- "What is the smallest version of this that someone would pay real money for this week?"
- "What are the 3 strongest competitors or alternatives — including 'do nothing,' spreadsheets, agencies, and manual work?"
- "What changed recently that makes this possible or urgent now?"
- "What is the single biggest reason this fails?"

## What investors actually fund in 2026

The bar has moved. Thin AI wrappers are dead. Bessemer: vertical AI is bigger than vertical SaaS because it taps the labor line, not the IT line.

**Hot categories:**
- **AI-native service companies** — don't sell software, sell the service. Replace the accountant, insurance broker, compliance officer.
- **SaaS challengers** — AI collapsed cost of building software 10–100x. Go after ERPs, industrial control, chip design, supply chain.
- **Software for agents** — every major software category needs rebuilding for AI agents as first-class users. APIs, MCPs, CLIs instead of buttons.
- **Voice AI agents** — not chatbots, but voice agents handling whole workflows: booking, intake, dispatch, clinical interactions.
- **Physical-world AI and robotics** — factories, construction, supply chains, agriculture.
- **Defense and energy** — counter-swarm defense, autonomous drones, electro-industrial infrastructure.

> Use investor taste as **inspiration**, not a checklist. Don't build because a trend report says it's hot. Build because you understand a specific pain better than the market does.

## How angels actually decide

Angels write checks in 15 minutes of conversation. Decision order: **team → idea as evidence of team quality → wedge → deal.**

> "At the seed stage, you are not investing in an idea. You are investing in a person's ability to figure things out." — Calacanis

**What "team" means to an angel:**
- Founder-market fit — have you lived this problem?
- Speed of learning under pressure (the founder who says "good point, here's how I'd test it Monday" beats the defender)
- Founder-distribution fit — can you reach buyers nobody else can?

**What angels disqualify in 60 seconds:**
- Vague "we're exploring AI in [space]"
- CTO who can't describe what they shipped last week
- Founder-idea mismatch (generalist pitching radiology AI, no radiologist co-founder)
- "We're going after the entire $50B legal market" with no first customer named

**Three moves that make an angel lean forward:**
1. Be specific in the first 15 seconds: "We're the scheduling layer for ambulatory surgery centers in Germany that still run on Excel and fax."
2. Install founder-market fit in the first 30 seconds: "I ran ops at one of these surgery centers."
3. Bring one piece of non-copyable evidence: a customer screenshot, a demo nobody else could have built, a proprietary insight.

## Pitch structure

**First minute prep:**
- First sentence: buyer + painful workflow + what you built
- Team edge: why your team understands this better than anyone else in the room
- Evidence: one real signal, even if small
- Weakest point: name the biggest risk yourself, say what you'd test next
