# Yarnia — 3-minute pitch script

> For the live prelim (3 min, no Q&A) and final (3 min + 2 min Q&A). Burhan delivers the opener (founder-fit). Keep the demo at the center: the **memory line** is the one thing that wins. Total spoken target ~2:40, leaving ~20s of slack for the demo to breathe.

---

## 0:00 — Opener (before slide 1, Burhan, to the room not the screen)
> "I'm Burhan, a father of two daughters. Bedtime is the one ritual I refuse to outsource to a screen. So we built the opposite of a screen."

*(Beat. Advance to Slide 1.)*

## 0:15 — Slide 1 · Problem + customer
> "It's 8pm. The kid won't settle. And every parent in this room knows the next move: you reach for the iPad you'll regret in the morning. That parent — of a three to seven year old — exists every single night. Their only options today are a glowing screen, or fighting bedtime alone."

## 0:40 — Slide 2 · Solution + product → DEMO
> "Yarnia is the third option. You open it, the screen goes dark, and a voice tells your kid a story made just for them. Watch."

*(Run the demo. Screen dims → voice greets the child by name → the memory line:)*
> **"Another dragon story like last time? I'll keep the thunder out."**

> "That line is the whole company. It opened already knowing this child — dragons, and no thunder, because it remembers her. A recorded catalog can never say that."

## 1:25 — Slide 3 · Why now
> "Why couldn't this exist two years ago? Three things just landed: real-time expressive voice that actually sounds human, story generation that costs a fraction of a cent, and a cultural moment where parents want *less* screen, not more. 2026 is the first year all three are true."

## 1:50 — Slide 4 · Market + competition
> "Sleep apps — Moshi, Calm Kids, Yoto, Toniebox — all play the same recording for every kid. We don't. We're generative plus per-child memory: the kid is the hero, and it remembers them. That's a different product shape, and it's expensive for a catalog business to retrofit. Market is bottom-up: parents of young kids times eight euros a month."

## 2:15 — Slide 5 · Business + evidence
> "Eight euros a month — Calm is eight, Spotify is ten. And the demand isn't a guess. Before Yarnia existed, I ran this exact ritual by hand: a recurring ChatGPT bedtime saga for my daughters, Finn and Ella. Ten-plus stories, same world, over six months — because the memory is what made them come back. We didn't invent the behavior. We lived it. Signups are live at yarnia.quest right now."

## 2:40 — Slides 6–7 · GTM + team + close
> "Every story ends with 'send it to grandma' — so the product is its own distribution. We're two engineers, one of us lives the problem. Our honest open question is whether parents trust an AI to tell their kid a story — so we built safety in from day one, and our next test is twenty parents this week. That's Yarnia: the opposite of a screen."

---

## If the live demo fails (decide in 3 seconds)
Cut to the **60–90s backup video** without apologizing: "Here's tonight's story, recorded an hour ago." Never debug on stage.

## The one thing to protect
The **memory line on open** is the pitch. If everything else slips, that moment must land cleanly. Seed "Lisa" (or Finn & Ella) so it's reliable.

---

## Q&A prep (finals only — 2 min)

**"Moshi / Yoto already own kids' bedtime audio."**
> "They're pre-recorded catalogs and fixed cards — the same story for every child. We're generative with per-child memory. The kid is the hero and it remembers them. That's not a feature they toggle on; it's a different product shape, and a risky one for a curated kid-safety brand to rebuild."

**"Won't co-creation hype the kid up right at bedtime?"**
> "Real risk. So co-creation is bounded to a quick, low-key setup at the start, then it goes fully calm and passive for the narration. Wind-down is preserved by design — we name this on purpose."

**"Consumer CAC is brutal. How do you acquire cheaply?"**
> "The share loop is built into the product: every story can be sent to grandma, who hears it and becomes the top of the next funnel. Plus parent communities and daycare partnerships. We're not buying attention — the story carries itself."

**"What's the moat once OpenAI ships a bedtime mode?"**
> "The per-child memory layer. After a few nights Yarnia knows your kid — what they love, what scares them, what helped them fall asleep. Leaving means starting over. The longer you use it, the more it costs to switch. That data compounds; a general assistant starts cold every time."

**"How is the AI safe for a 4-year-old?"**
> "Every story passes through age and safety constraints before a word is spoken, and it actively avoids the specific things that scare each child — because it remembers their fears. Safety isn't a filter we added; it's the trust the whole product is built on."

**"Why you two?"**
> "Founder-market fit: Burhan is a dad of two who already hand-built this ritual for years before any product existed. And we're a rare all-engineer team — we shipped the working product today, not just slides."
