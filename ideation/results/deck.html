✦ Yarnia · the opposite of a screen
The customer · the problem

It's 8pm. The kid won't settle.
The parent reaches for the iPad they'll regret.

  • Who: a parent of a 3–7 year old. Not "families broadly," but one person you can picture, who exists every single night.
  • Today they choose between two bad options: hand over a glowing screen (and the guilt), or fight bedtime alone.
  • The one ritual parents most want to protect is the one most colonized by screens.
The product · live demo

Yarnia: a screen-off voice that knows your child.

  • Open it → the screen goes dark → a warm voice greets your child by name.
  • A quick, low-key co-creation ("an owl or a dragon tonight?"), then it narrates a calm, personalized story to sleep.
  • Every story is saved. Send tonight's to grandma with one tap.

The moment that wins

"Another dragon story like last time? I'll keep the thunder out."

It opens already knowing the child. That single line is the whole pitch, and the one thing a recorded catalog can't say.

Live demo on the laptop · a working app (per-child memory, content-safety, a live subscribe flow) on Flutter + Cloudflare + InstantDB + ElevenLabs.
Why now

This is only possible in 2026.

Expressive voice

Real-time, emotional TTS (ElevenLabs) finally sounds like a person telling a story, not a robot reading one. A year ago this broke the spell.

Cheap generation

The anti-attention turn

Parents in 2026 actively want less screen, not more. The cultural moment finally rewards a product that turns the screen off.

Business model · evidence

€8/mo, and the behavior is already real.

  • Model: €8/mo subscription. Calm is €8, Spotify €10. A price below the "do I need this" threshold, and the subscribe flow is already built, not a mockup.
  • Trust as a feature: every story runs through age + safety constraints and avoids the exact things that scare your kid, because it remembers them.
  • Live signup page at yarnia.quest with a real-time waitlist counter, built to share into parent communities.

Go-to-market

The story sells the next parent.

1 · The share loop

Every story ends with "send tonight's story to grandma." Grandma hears it → a new parent lands at the top of the funnel. Distribution is built into the product, not bolted on.

2 · Where parents already gather

Parent communities (r/Parenting, local Hamburg/DE groups) and preschool / daycare partnerships. Naming a real channel, not "we'll do ads."

The moat compounds: the longer a family uses Yarnia, the more it knows each child (every kid gets their own remembered world), and the more starting over costs.
